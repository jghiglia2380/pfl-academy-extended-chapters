## Overview

L-54 examines two critical macroeconomic challenges that directly affect personal finances: inflation (rising prices eroding purchasing power) and unemployment (inability to find work despite seeking it). Students learn how inflation is measured and caused, types of unemployment, the Phillips Curve trade-off, and strategies to protect purchasing power and navigate job markets in different economic conditions.

### Core Economic Concepts

**Inflation:**
- Definition: Sustained increase in general price level over time
- Measurement: Consumer Price Index (CPI) tracks basket of goods
- Demand-pull: Too much money chasing too few goods (excess demand)
- Cost-push: Rising production costs passed to consumers (supply shock)
- Monetary: Central bank increases money supply too rapidly
- Effects: Erodes purchasing power, benefits borrowers, hurts savers

**Unemployment:**
- Frictional: Temporary while transitioning between jobs (normal, healthy)
- Structural: Skills don't match available jobs (requires retraining)
- Cyclical: Recession reduces labor demand (macroeconomic problem)
- Natural rate: Frictional + structural (unavoidable minimum)
- Effects: Lost income, skill deterioration, psychological costs

**Phillips Curve:**
- Historical trade-off: Lower unemployment → higher inflation
- Policy dilemma: Can't minimize both simultaneously
- Stagflation (1970s): High inflation + high unemployment (curve broke down)
- Modern view: Short-run trade-off, no long-run trade-off

## Core Concepts

**Key Insights:**
- Inflation erodes purchasing power: $100 at 3% inflation = $74 in 10 years
- Real returns matter more than nominal (adjust for inflation)
- Different unemployment types require different solutions
- Structural unemployment risk varies by career field (automation threat)
- Wage negotiations should account for inflation expectations

**Decision Framework:**
When making financial decisions during inflation/unemployment, ask:
- What's the current inflation rate and trend?
- Are my wages/savings keeping pace with inflation?
- What assets protect against inflation (TIPS, real estate, stocks)?
- Is unemployment in my field frictional, structural, or cyclical?
- Should I invest in skills that reduce structural unemployment risk?
