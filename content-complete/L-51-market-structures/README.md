### Core Market Structures

**Perfect Competition:**
- Many sellers, identical products, no barriers to entry
- No individual seller can influence price (price takers)
- Examples: Agricultural commodities, stock market
- Consumer strategy: Focus on convenience and service (prices identical)

**Monopolistic Competition:**
- Many sellers, differentiated products, low entry barriers
- Some pricing power from brand differentiation
- Examples: Restaurants, clothing stores, coffee shops
- Consumer strategy: Compare value, seek discounts, consider alternatives

**Oligopoly:**
- Few sellers (3-10), high barriers to entry
- Significant pricing power, strategic interdependence
- Examples: Airlines, cell phones, automobiles
- Consumer strategy: Time purchases, compare limited options, watch for price competition

**Monopoly:**
- Single seller, very high barriers, unique product
- Maximum pricing power (limited by regulation or demand)
- Examples: Utilities, local cable, patented drugs
- Consumer strategy: Accept price or seek substitutes; advocate for regulation

## Core Concepts

**Key Insights:**
- Market structure determines the balance of power between buyers and sellers
- More competition generally means lower prices and more consumer surplus
- Product differentiation creates pricing power even with many sellers
- Barriers to entry protect incumbent firms from competition
- Monopolies may be natural (utilities) or artificial (patents, regulations)

**Decision Framework:**
Before major purchases, ask:
- How many sellers compete in this market?
- How differentiated are the products (can I easily substitute)?
- How much pricing power do sellers have?
- What's my best strategy given this market structure?
- Are there barriers preventing new competitors from entering?

## Content Highlights

- Market power spectrum: Perfect competition (zero power) to monopoly (maximum power)
- Barriers to entry: Economies of scale, patents, regulations, network effects, capital requirements
- Consumer surplus varies dramatically by structure: Most in perfect competition, least in monopoly
- Strategic pricing in oligopolies: Price leadership, tacit collusion, periodic price wars
- When monopolies may be efficient: Natural monopolies (utilities), temporary monopolies (patents incentivize innovation)
