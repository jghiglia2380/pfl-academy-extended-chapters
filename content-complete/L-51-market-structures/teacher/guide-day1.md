# L-51: Market Structures and Consumer Choice
## Teacher Guide - Day 1

### Lesson Overview
Students learn the four main market structures, understand how competition level affects pricing and consumer surplus, and recognize market structures in their daily purchases.

**Total Time:** 55 minutes
**Format:** Direct instruction with examples and discussion
**Key Concept:** Market structure (number of sellers and barriers to entry) determines seller pricing power and consumer surplus

## Detailed Facilitation Guide

### Opening Activity (5 minutes)

**Objective:** Establish that market structure affects prices and consumer power

**Facilitation Steps:**
1. Display question: "Why does Netflix cost what it costs?"
2. "Why does your electricity in {{STATE_NAME}} cost what it costs?"
3. Take 2-3 student responses
4. Guide toward insight: "Different markets have different numbers of sellers and levels of competition"

**Expected Responses:**
- Netflix: "Lots of competitors (Disney+, Hulu, HBO), prices competitive"
- Electricity: "Only one provider, they set the price"

**Transition:** "Today we'll learn how to classify markets and what that means for your wallet"

**Connection to Previous Chapters:**
- "L-48: Market economies rely on competition"
- "L-50: Supply and demand work best with many competitive sellers"
- "Today: What happens when competition is limited?"

---

### Four Market Structures Overview (10 minutes)

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part 1: Framework Introduction (3 minutes)**
1. Write on board: "Market Structure = Number of Sellers + Barriers to Entry"
2. Create table with 4 structures:

| Structure | # Sellers | Product | Entry | Price Control |
|-----------|-----------|---------|-------|---------------|
| Perfect Competition | Many | Identical | None | None |
| Monopolistic Comp. | Many | Different | Low | Some |
| Oligopoly | Few (3-10) | Either | High | Significant |
| Monopoly | One | Unique | Very High | Maximum |

**Part 2: Consumer Surplus Spectrum (3 minutes)**
Draw spectrum on board:
```
High Consumer Surplus ←————————————→ Low Consumer Surplus
(Good for you)                     (Bad for you)

Perfect → Monopolistic → Oligopoly → Monopoly
```

**Key Point:** "As competition decreases, seller power increases and you pay more"

**Part 3: Real-World Examples (4 minutes)**
For each structure, give 1-2 quick examples:
- **Perfect:** Wheat, stocks, farmer's markets
- **Monopolistic:** Restaurants, clothing stores, coffee shops
- **Oligopoly:** Cell phones, airlines, auto manufacturers
- **Monopoly:** Your local electric company, water service

**Check for Understanding:**
"Thumbs up if you can name the four structures. Sideways if you need review."

---

### Perfect Competition (5 minutes)

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part 1: Define and Explain (3 minutes)**
1. "Many sellers, identical product, no barriers, no price control"
2. **Key Insight:** "Sellers are price TAKERS, not price MAKERS"
3. "If one farmer charges more for wheat, buyers go elsewhere"

**Example: Farmer's Market**
- "10 vendors selling tomatoes"
- "All tomatoes roughly same quality"
- "What happens if one vendor charges $4/lb and others charge $3/lb?"
  - (Nobody buys from expensive vendor)
- "What happens if one vendor charges $2/lb?"
  - (Everyone buys from that vendor until sold out)
- "Result: All vendors charge about $3/lb (market price)"

**Part 2: Why Rare in Practice (2 minutes)**
Ask class: "Why don't we see perfect competition everywhere?"
- Product differentiation is easy (branding, location, service)
- Some barriers always exist
- Firms actively avoid perfect competition (want pricing power)

**Connection to L-50:**
"Remember supply and demand curves? Perfect competition is where they work most purely. Price = equilibrium, no seller can change it."

**Personal Finance Application:**
"When buying commoditized goods (gas, generic drugs), shop by price alone. No quality difference."

---

### Monopolistic Competition (5 minutes)

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part 1: Define and Explain (3 minutes)**
1. "Many sellers, differentiated products, low barriers, some price control"
2. **Key Insight:** "Differentiation creates mini-monopolies"
3. "Starbucks has a monopoly on the Starbucks experience"

**Example: Coffee Shops**
- Draw on board:
  - Starbucks: $5
  - Local cafe: $4
  - Dunkin': $3
  - Home-brewed: $0.50
- "All provide caffeine. Why do prices differ?"
  - (Atmosphere, convenience, brand, quality perception)
- "You're paying for differentiation"

**Part 2: Long-Run Equilibrium (2 minutes)**
1. "If restaurants are profitable, new ones open"
2. "More competition → prices stabilize"
3. "Long-run: Economic profit approaches zero (like perfect competition)"
4. "But firms survive through differentiation"

**Discussion:**
"When is brand premium worth it? When isn't it?"
- Take 2 student examples
- Guide toward: "Depends on what YOU value"

**Personal Finance Application:**
"In monopolistically competitive markets: Shop around, ignore marketing hype, decide what you actually value"

---

### Oligopoly (10 minutes)

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part 1: Define and Explain (4 minutes)**
1. "Few sellers (3-10), high barriers, significant price control"
2. **Key Characteristic:** "Strategic interdependence"
3. "Each firm's decision affects competitors"

**Example: Cell Phone Carriers in {{STATE_NAME}}**
- "{{STATE_TELECOM_PROVIDERS}} dominate (typically AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile)"
- "If Verizon lowers prices, others must respond"
- "If all three keep prices high, all profit"
- "Hard for new competitor to enter (need billions in infrastructure)"

**Part 2: Price Leadership (2 minutes)**
1. "Often largest firm sets prices, others follow"
2. "Example: Airlines and baggage fees"
   - "One airline adds $25 baggage fee"
   - "Within weeks, all major airlines match"
   - "Why? Don't want to be only cheap airline (race to bottom)"

**Part 3: Barriers to Entry (2 minutes)**
Ask class: "Why can't you start a cell phone company?"
- Expected: "Too expensive" "Need licenses" "Established brands"
- List barriers:
  - Capital requirements (billions)
  - Economies of scale
  - Government regulation (spectrum licenses)
  - Brand loyalty

**Part 4: Consumer Impact (2 minutes)**
**Advantages:**
- Stable prices
- Heavy investment in quality
- Nationwide networks

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher prices than competitive markets
- Limited choices
- Potential for collusion

**Connection to L-49:**
"Remember incentives? Oligopolists face incentive to cooperate (keep prices high) vs. compete (undercut rivals). Balance determines prices."

**Personal Finance Application:**
"With oligopolies: Compare all options, negotiate at renewal, threaten to switch"

**Real-World Skepticism:**
"When all airlines raise prices together, ask: Is this coincidence or coordination?"

---

### Monopoly (10 minutes)

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part 1: Define and Explain (3 minutes)**
1. "One seller, no substitutes, complete price control"
2. **Key Point:** "Monopolist sets price to maximize profit, not competitive equilibrium"
3. "Restricts output to raise price"

**Example: {{STATE_NAME}} Electricity**
- "You likely have one provider: {{STATE_UTILITY_PROVIDERS}}"
- "No choice: pay their rate or go without electricity"
- "They set price as high as regulators allow"

**Part 2: Types of Monopolies (4 minutes)**

**Natural Monopoly:**
- "One firm can serve entire market more efficiently than multiple firms"
- "Example: Electricity, water, gas"
- "Why: Massive infrastructure (imagine 3 sets of power lines to every house - wasteful)"
- "Government response: Regulate prices"

**Legal Monopoly:**
- "Government grants exclusive right"
- "Example: Patents on drugs (20 years), copyrights"
- "Why: Encourage innovation (reward for R&D)"
- "Time-limited"

**Market Power Monopoly:**
- "Firm dominates through business practices"
- "Historical: Standard Oil, AT&T before breakup"
- "Current debates: Google (search), Facebook (social), Amazon (e-commerce)"
- "Government response: Antitrust enforcement"

**Part 3: Deadweight Loss (3 minutes)**
Draw simple diagram on board:
```
Price
  S
  |  /\
  |/   \
  |  \ / Deadweight
  | \/  Loss
  |___\___
```

Explain:
- "Monopoly charges P_m (high price)"
- "Produces Q_m (low quantity)"
- "Competitive market would charge P_c (lower) and produce Q_c (higher)"
- "Deadweight loss = transactions that should happen but don't (inefficient)"

**Key Teaching Point:**
"Monopolies are bad for society: high prices, low quantity, inefficiency. That's why government regulates them."

**Discussion:**
"Are monopolies ever good?"
- Natural monopolies (if regulated)
- Patents (encourage innovation)
- But unregulated monopolies almost always harm consumers

**Personal Finance Application:**
"With monopolies: Minimize consumption, look for long-term substitutes, advocate for regulation"

## Differentiation Strategies

**For Struggling Students:**
- Focus on two structures (perfect competition vs. monopoly) before introducing middle ground
- Use concrete examples they experience (their actual cell phone bill, electricity bill)
- Provide pre-made classification tables

**For Advanced Students:**
- Discuss game theory (prisoner's dilemma in oligopolies)
- Analyze historical antitrust cases (Standard Oil, AT&T breakup, Microsoft)
- Calculate deadweight loss numerically

**For ELL Students:**
- Pre-teach vocabulary: monopoly, oligopoly, barrier, substitute
- Use visual aids heavily (graphs, photos of companies)
- Provide bilingual glossary

## Common Misconceptions

**Misconception 1:** "Monopoly = any big company"
- **Correct:** "Monopoly = only seller with no close substitutes. Amazon is big but faces competition."

**Misconception 2:** "Perfect competition is ideal"
- **Nuance:** "Perfect competition minimizes prices but provides no variety and little innovation. Trade-offs exist."

**Misconception 3:** "Oligopolies illegally collude"
- **Clarify:** "Explicit collusion is illegal. But tacit coordination (watching competitors, price leadership) is common and legal."

**Misconception 4:** "Government always opposes monopolies"
- **Correct:** "Natural monopolies are accepted but regulated. Patents are temporary legal monopolies to encourage innovation."
