# L-50: Supply and Demand in Personal Finance
## Teacher Guide - Day 1

### Lesson Overview
Students learn the laws of supply and demand, how markets reach equilibrium, and what causes supply/demand curves to shift. Heavy emphasis on {{STATE_NAME}} applications and personal finance relevance.

**Total Time:** 55 minutes
**Format:** Direct instruction with interactive examples and discussion
**Key Concept:** Supply and demand determine market prices; understanding these forces helps make better financial decisions

### Law of Demand (5 minutes)

**Objective:** Students understand inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part 1: State the Law (1 minute)**
1. Write on board: **"Law of Demand: Price ↑ → Quantity Demanded ↓"**
2. "As price increases, quantity demanded decreases, all else being equal"
3. "This is called an 'inverse' or 'negative' relationship"

**Part 2: Three Reasons WHY (3 minutes)**
1. **Substitution Effect:**
   - "When beef prices rise, what do you buy instead?" (Chicken, pasta, beans)
   - "When {{STATE_NAME}} rent rises, what do people do?" (Move further out, get roommates, leave state)

2. **Income Effect:**
   - "If gas goes from $3 to $5/gallon, what happens to your buying power?" (Decreases)
   - "Your nominal income is same, but real income fell—you can afford less of everything"

3. **Diminishing Marginal Utility:**
   - "How much do you want the first slice of pizza?" (A lot!)
   - "How about the fifth slice?" (Not as much)
   - "You'll only buy more slices if price is lower, because each additional slice is worth less to you"

**Part 3: Visual Representation (1 minute)**
Draw simple demand curve on board:
```
Price
  ↑
  |  ╲
  |    ╲
  |      ╲
  |________╲___→ Quantity
```

"Notice: downward sloping. Higher price = lower quantity"

**Check for Understanding:**
- "Thumbs up if you can explain why demand slopes downward. Sideways if you're still processing."
- Cold call 1 student: "Give me an example of the substitution effect from your own life."

**Common Misconception:**
**"Demand means how much people want something."**
- **Correct:** "Demand is the relationship between price and quantity—how much people will buy at each price. 'Wanting' something isn't demand unless you're willing and able to pay for it."

### Law of Supply (5 minutes)

**Objective:** Students understand positive relationship between price and quantity supplied

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part 1: State the Law (1 minute)**
1. Write on board: **"Law of Supply: Price ↑ → Quantity Supplied ↑"**
2. "As price increases, quantity supplied increases, all else being equal"
3. "This is a 'positive' or 'direct' relationship"

**Part 2: Three Reasons WHY (3 minutes)**
1. **Profit Motive:**
   - "If housing prices double in {{STATE_NAME}}, what do developers do?" (Build more)
   - "Higher price = higher profit → businesses produce more"

2. **New Entrants:**
   - "When hand sanitizer prices hit $15, who started making it?" (Distilleries, breweries, new companies)
   - "High prices attract new producers to the market"

3. **Opportunity Cost:**
   - "Remember L-49? Producers face opportunity costs"
   - "If oil is $100/barrel, it's worth drilling in difficult/expensive locations"
   - "If oil is $30/barrel, only easy drilling makes sense"
   - "Higher prices justify higher costs of production"

**Part 3: Visual Representation (1 minute)**
Draw supply curve on board:
```
Price
  ↑       ╱
  |     ╱
  |   ╱
  | ╱
  |_______→ Quantity
```

"Notice: upward sloping. Higher price = higher quantity supplied"

**Real-World Example:**
"{{STATE_NAME}} example: When median home prices hit {{STATE_MEDIAN_HOME_PRICE}}, developers are building [more/fewer] new homes. Why? Because at these prices, it's profitable."

**Monitoring:**
- Are students connecting supply to producers' incentives?
- Do they see how opportunity cost (L-49) relates to supply curve?

### Market Equilibrium (10 minutes)

**Objective:** Students understand how markets reach equilibrium and what happens at disequilibrium prices

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part 1: Define Equilibrium (2 minutes)**
1. **Write on board:** "Equilibrium: The price where Quantity Demanded = Quantity Supplied"
2. "At this price, the market 'clears'—everyone who wants to buy can find a seller, everyone who wants to sell can find a buyer"
3. "No shortages, no surpluses"

**Part 2: Equilibrium Example - Used Cars (5 minutes)**

Create table on board:

| Price | Qd | Qs | Result |
|-------|----|----|--------|
| $30,000 | 100 | 400 | Surplus (300) |
| $25,000 | 200 | 300 | Surplus (100) |
| **$20,000** | **300** | **300** | **Equilibrium** |
| $15,000 | 400 | 200 | Shortage (200) |
| $10,000 | 500 | 100 | Shortage (400) |

**Facilitate Analysis:**

1. **At $30,000 (above equilibrium):**
   - Ask: "How many cars do sellers want to sell?" (400)
   - "How many do buyers want?" (100)
   - "What's the result?" (Surplus of 300)
   - "What do sellers do?" (Lower prices to sell excess inventory)
   - "Where does price move?" (Down toward equilibrium)

2. **At $10,000 (below equilibrium):**
   - Ask: "How many cars do buyers want?" (500)
   - "How many are sellers offering?" (100)
   - "What's the result?" (Shortage of 400)
   - "What do buyers do?" (Bid up prices to secure cars)
   - "Where does price move?" (Up toward equilibrium)

3. **At $20,000 (equilibrium):**
   - "Supply = Demand. Market clears. No pressure for price to change."

**Part 3: Visual (3 minutes)**
Draw supply and demand curves intersecting:
```
Price
  ↑       S
  |      ╱╲
  |    ╱    ╲
  |  ╱        ╲ D
  |╱____________╲→ Quantity
      E (equilibrium)
```

**Key Teaching Points:**
- "Markets naturally move toward equilibrium"
- "If price is too high, surpluses push it down"
- "If price is too low, shortages push it up"
- "This happens automatically—no central planner needed" (Connect to L-48!)

**Class Discussion:**
"Why might a market NOT reach equilibrium?"
- Price controls (minimum wage, rent control)
- Slow adjustment (housing takes time to build)
- Information problems (buyers/sellers don't know true value)

**Connection to L-48:**
"Remember the price mechanism from L-48? This IS the price mechanism in action. Prices coordinate millions of individual decisions without anyone being in charge."

### Shifts in Supply and Demand (15 minutes)

**Objective:** Students can identify factors that shift curves and predict effects on equilibrium

**Critical Distinction:**
- **Movement ALONG curve:** Price changes
- **SHIFT of curve:** Something OTHER than price changes

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part 1: Demand Shifters (7 minutes)**

1. **Write on board: 5 Demand Shifters:**
   - Income
   - Prices of related goods
   - Tastes/preferences
   - Expectations
   - Number of buyers

2. **Go through each with examples:**

**Income:**
- "You get a raise. What happens to your demand for restaurants?" (Increases—shifts right)
- "Recession hits. Demand for luxury goods?" (Decreases—shifts left)
- Draw demand curve shifting right

**Prices of Related Goods:**
- **Substitutes:**
  - "Beef price doubles. What happens to demand for chicken?" (Increases)
  - "Gas price spikes. Demand for electric cars?" (Increases)
- **Complements:**
  - "Gas price spikes. Demand for SUVs?" (Decreases)
  - "Coffee price falls. Demand for cream/sugar?" (Increases)

**Tastes/Preferences:**
- "TikTok makes product go viral. Demand?" (Increases)
- "Health study says product is harmful. Demand?" (Decreases)

**Expectations:**
- "You think housing prices will rise. Demand for homes now?" (Increases—buy before prices rise)
- "You expect recession. Demand for big purchases?" (Decreases—delay)

**Number of Buyers:**
- "{{STATE_NAME}} population growing. Demand for housing?" (Increases)

3. **{{STATE_NAME}} Housing Example:**
   - "{{STATE_NAME}}'s economy is booming"
   - Ask class: "What happens to demand for housing?" (Shifts right)
   - "Why?" (Income ↑, population ↑, expectations of future increases)
   - "Result?" (Prices rise)
   - Draw on board: Demand shifts right → new higher equilibrium price

**Part 2: Supply Shifters (8 minutes)**

1. **Write on board: 6 Supply Shifters:**
   - Input costs
   - Technology
   - Number of sellers
   - Expectations
   - Government policy
   - Natural events/shocks

2. **Go through each with examples:**

**Input Costs:**
- "Lumber prices double. Supply of new homes?" (Decreases—shifts left)
- "{{STATE_NAME}} raises minimum wage. Supply of restaurant meals?" (May decrease—higher labor costs)

**Technology:**
- "Automation improves. Supply?" (Increases)
- "3D printing allows custom manufacturing. Supply?" (Increases)

**Number of Sellers:**
- "New apartment buildings completed in {{STATE_NAME}}. Supply of rentals?" (Increases)
- "Businesses close during recession. Supply?" (Decreases)

**Expectations:**
- "Oil producers expect higher future prices. Supply now?" (Decreases—hold back)
- "Farmers expect bad harvest next year. Supply this year?" (Increase—sell now)

**Government Policy:**
- "Taxes on production increase. Supply?" (Decreases—shifts left)
- "Subsidies for solar panels. Supply?" (Increases)
- "Strict regulations. Supply?" (Usually decreases—compliance costs)

**Natural Events:**
- "Drought. Supply of crops?" (Decreases)
- "Pandemic. Supply chain disruptions. Supply?" (Decreases)
- "Perfect weather. Supply of crops?" (Increases)

3. **{{STATE_NAME}} Gasoline Example:**
   - "Refinery in {{STATE_NAME}} shuts down for maintenance"
   - Ask: "Supply or demand shift?" (Supply)
   - "Direction?" (Left—decreases)
   - "Effect on price?" (Increases)
   - "Effect on quantity?" (Decreases)
   - Draw on board: Supply shifts left → higher price, lower quantity

**Key Teaching Moment:**
"Every time you see a price change in the real world, ask: 'Is this a supply shift or demand shift?' Being able to identify the cause helps you predict how long the change will last and whether it will reverse."

**Assessment Opportunity:**
Quick scenarios for students to identify:
1. "New iPhone released. Demand or supply shift?" (Demand right—tastes)
2. "Factory fire reduces production. Supply or demand?" (Supply left)
3. "Tax cut gives people more income. Supply or demand?" (Demand right)

### Real-World Applications (10 minutes)

**Objective:** Apply concepts to personal finance decisions

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Application 1: Pandemic Hand Sanitizer (Revisit from L-48) (3 minutes)**

Walk through step-by-step:

1. **March 2020: Demand Shock**
   - "Pandemic hits. Everyone wants hand sanitizer. Demand shifts?"
   - (Massively right)
   - "At old price ($3), what happens?" (Huge shortage)

2. **Price Adjustment**
   - "Retailers raise prices to $12-15. Why?" (Eliminate shortage, ration scarce supply)
   - "Some people call this 'price gouging.' But what's happening economically?" (Market reaching new equilibrium at higher price)

3. **Supply Response**
   - "High prices signal what to producers?" (Profit opportunity)
   - "Who enters market?" (Distilleries, breweries, new companies)
   - "Supply shifts?" (Right)

4. **New Equilibrium**
   - "Eventually supply catches up. Prices?" (Fall back down to $4-5)
   - "Still higher than before (demand elevated) but much lower than peak"

**Lesson:** "Markets self-correct through price signals. High prices attract new supply. This takes time, but it works."

**Application 2: {{STATE_NAME}} Housing Market (4 minutes)**

1. **Current Situation:**
   - "Median home price: {{STATE_MEDIAN_HOME_PRICE}}"
   - "Why are prices at this level?"

2. **Demand Analysis:**
   - Ask class: "What factors are increasing housing demand in {{STATE_NAME}}?"
   - Expected answers: Population growth, rising incomes, low interest rates
   - "Demand shifts?" (Right)

3. **Supply Analysis:**
   - "What's happening to housing supply?"
   - Expected answers: Construction costs rising, zoning restrictions, limited land
   - "Supply shifts?" (Left or not keeping up)

4. **Result:**
   - "When demand shifts right and supply shifts left (or doesn't keep up), prices?" (Rise significantly)
   - "Evidence?" (Bidding wars, homes selling above asking, low inventory)

**Personal Finance Application:**
- "If you're planning to buy in {{STATE_NAME}}, what does this analysis tell you?"
  - Prices likely to keep rising (supply/demand imbalance)
  - Buy sooner rather than later if you can afford it
  - Or consider less competitive markets

**Application 3: Career Planning Using Labor Market Supply/Demand (3 minutes)**

1. **High Demand + Limited Supply = Rising Wages:**
   - "Healthcare in {{STATE_NAME}}: Aging population (demand ↑), limited med school spots (supply constrained)"
   - "Tech: Digital transformation (demand ↑), but lots of coding bootcamps (supply ↑ too)"
   - "Skilled trades: Infrastructure needs (demand ↑), workers retiring (supply ↓)"

2. **Research Your Career:**
   - "Before choosing a career, ask:"
     - "Is demand for this skill growing or shrinking?"
     - "How many people are entering this field (supply)?"
     - "What's the supply/demand balance?"

3. **{{STATE_NAME}} Specific:**
   - "What industries are growing in {{STATE_NAME}}?" ({{STATE_MAJOR_INDUSTRIES}})
   - "These create demand for specific skills. Position yourself accordingly."

**Key Insight:**
"Supply and demand aren't just abstract concepts. They determine:
- What you pay for everything
- How much you earn
- When to buy/sell assets
- Which careers offer best opportunities

Understanding these forces gives you an advantage in every financial decision."

## Differentiation Strategies

### For Struggling Students:
- Provide pre-drawn supply/demand graphs with examples
- Use only 2-3 demand shifters and 2-3 supply shifters (not all 5-6)
- Focus on concrete examples they experience (gas prices, concert tickets)
- Pair with stronger student for practice problems
- Offer sentence frames: "When ___ increases, demand shifts ___"

### For Advanced Students:
- Introduce elasticity concept early (preview from Day 2)
- Assign research: "Find supply and demand data for a specific market and graph it"
- Analyze: "Why don't markets always reach equilibrium? What prevents it?"
- Calculate: "If demand increases 20% and supply increases 10%, what happens to price?"
- Read: Excerpts from "Basic Economics" by Thomas Sowell

### For ELL Students:
- Pre-teach vocabulary: demand, supply, equilibrium, surplus, shortage, shift
- Provide bilingual glossary
- Use extensive visual aids (graphs, diagrams, photos)
- Encourage native language discussion before English reporting
- Pair with bilingual peer if available

### For Students with IEPs:
- Extended time on practice problems
- Provide partially completed graphs (students fill in missing parts)
- Audio recordings of key explanations
- Reduce number of examples (depth over breadth)
- Check in individually during practice

## Common Misconceptions & How to Address

**Misconception 1:** "High demand means people want it a lot"
- **Correct:** "Demand is the entire relationship between price and quantity—how much people will buy at each price. 'Want' without ability/willingness to pay isn't demand."

**Misconception 2:** "Supply means how much exists"
- **Correct:** "Supply is the relationship between price and quantity producers will offer at each price. Inventory is a quantity, not supply."

**Misconception 3:** "Movement along the curve is the same as shifting the curve"
- **Clarify:** "Price changes cause movement ALONG curves. Non-price factors SHIFT the curves. This is crucial!"

**Misconception 4:** "Equilibrium is always 'good'"
- **Nuance:** "Equilibrium just means market clears. But the equilibrium might be at a very high price (housing crisis) or very low wage (oversaturated labor market). Equilibrium ≠ fair or desirable."

**Misconception 5:** "Supply and demand only apply to products"
- **Expand:** "Supply and demand apply to labor markets (you supply your work), housing markets (you demand a place to live), financial markets (supply/demand for loans), and more."

## Connection to Other L-Chapters

**From L-48 (Economic Systems):**
- Price mechanism coordinates market economies → Supply and demand IS the price mechanism
- Competition creates incentives → Suppliers compete for customers, driving toward equilibrium
- {{STATE_NAME}}'s mixed economy → Most prices determined by supply and demand

- Scarcity is fundamental problem → Supply curves show scarcity of resources
