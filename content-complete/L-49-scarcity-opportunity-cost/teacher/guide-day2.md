# L-49: Scarcity, Opportunity Cost, and Incentives
## Teacher Guide - Day 2: Learning Lab

### Lesson Overview
Students apply opportunity cost analysis to real-world decisions through hands-on activities. Heavy emphasis on calculation, comparison, and personal application to {{STATE_NAME}} context.

**Total Time:** 55 minutes
**Format:** 90% hands-on activities, 10% instruction
**Key Outcome:** Students can calculate opportunity costs for major financial decisions and design effective incentive systems for their goals

## Detailed Facilitation Guide

### Opening Reflection (5 minutes)

**Objective:** Prime students to apply concepts to their personal decisions

**Facilitation Steps:**
1. Display prompt: "Think of a significant financial decision you'll make in the next 1-5 years (college, career, car, housing, etc.)"
2. Set timer for 3 minutes of silent writing
3. "Write: What are you giving up by making that choice? What's the opportunity cost?"
4. Transition: "Keep this in mind. Today we'll build skills to analyze decisions like this systematically."

**Do NOT:**
- Have students share publicly (too personal for some)
- Judge their choices
- Rush this reflection

**Monitoring:**
- Circulate to ensure students are engaged (not daydreaming)
- Note which students identify good decision scenarios for Activity 1 coaching

---

### Activity 1: College vs. Alternative Paths Opportunity Cost Analysis (20 minutes)

**Objective:** Students calculate full economic cost (explicit + implicit) for three post-high school paths

**Setup:**
Students work individually first, then compare with partner

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Introduction (2 minutes):**
1. "You're analyzing Alex's decision: 4-year college vs. trade school vs. immediate workforce"
2. "For each path, you'll calculate explicit costs AND opportunity costs"
3. "Remember from yesterday: Total Economic Cost = Explicit Costs + Opportunity Costs"

**Independent Work (12 minutes):**
1. "Start with Path A: 4-year university. Complete the table."
2. Set timer for 4 minutes
3. Circulate and monitor:
   - Are students correctly identifying explicit costs?
   - Are they calculating opportunity costs (forgone earnings)?
   - Watch for math errors

**Key Calculations to Monitor:**

**Path A (4-Year University):**
- Explicit costs: Tuition × 4 + Books × 4 + Housing × 4
- Opportunity cost: ($30,000 full-time earnings - $12,000 part-time earnings) × 4 years = $72,000
- Total economic cost: Explicit + $72,000

**Common Student Errors:**
- Forgetting to subtract part-time earnings from opportunity cost
- Only calculating one year instead of four
- Not including housing if living at home (correct—it's not a new cost)

4. "Now complete Path B and Path C" (8 minutes)
5. "Finally, complete Part D: 10-year outlook"

**Facilitate Partner Discussion (5 minutes):**
1. "Turn to your partner. Compare your calculations."
2. "If you disagree, discuss why. Find the correct answer together."
3. Discussion prompts on board:
   - Which path has the highest opportunity cost in years 1-4? (University)
   - Which path generates the most wealth by year 10? (Likely university, but depends on specific numbers)
   - What factors beyond money matter?

**Whole Class Debrief (3 minutes):**
1. "Quick poll: Which path has highest 10-year net wealth?" (Take show of hands)
2. Call on 2-3 students to share their totals
3. Address any major calculation discrepancies
4. **Key Teaching Point:** "University has the highest short-term opportunity cost ($72K in forgone earnings) but might have the lowest long-term opportunity cost if it leads to significantly higher earnings. However, this depends on:
   - Field of study
   - Completion of degree
   - Career outcomes
   - Individual circumstances
   - Personal fit and motivation"

**Differentiation:**
- **Struggling students:** Provide partially completed tables with some calculations filled in
- **Advanced students:** Add Path D: High-earning trade (electrician, plumber) with starting salary of $45K after 2-year apprenticeship, rising to $75K by year 10
- **Students who finish early:** "Calculate break-even point: In which year does the college grad's cumulative earnings surpass the other paths?"

**Assessment Opportunity:**
Are students correctly calculating opportunity costs? Check their work during circulation. Identify students who need additional support for Activity 2.

### Activity 2: Monthly Budget Opportunity Cost Analysis (15 minutes)

**Objective:** Apply opportunity cost thinking to everyday spending decisions

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Introduction (2 minutes):**
1. "Scenario: You earn $1,200/month. Required expenses are $330. You have $870 discretionary income."
2. "Your task: Allocate the $870 and analyze the opportunity cost of each choice."
3. "There's no one right answer—it depends on your goals and values."

**Independent Work (10 minutes):**
1. "Complete the Budget Allocation Table. Decide how much to save vs. spend."
2. Set timer for 5 minutes
3. Circulate and monitor:
   - Are students allocating exactly $870? (Not more, not less)
   - Are they identifying opportunity costs?
   - Do their allocations align with stated goals?

**Common Student Approaches:**
- **Savers:** Allocate heavily to car savings, emergency fund, college (low entertainment)
- **Spenders:** Higher entertainment, shopping (lower savings)
- **Balanced:** Mix of both

**All are valid if students can articulate the trade-offs!**

4. "Now answer the Analysis Questions 1-4" (5 minutes)

**Key Insights to Listen For:**
- "$300/month to car savings = $3,600/year to entertainment forgone"
- "Emergency fund provides security but delays other goals"
- "50/30/20 rule is a guideline, not a law—my priorities differ because..."

**Partner Comparison (3 minutes):**
1. "Share your allocation with a partner"
2. "Explain: What are you prioritizing? What trade-offs did you accept?"
3. "Discuss: How are your opportunity costs different based on your goals?"

**No Whole-Class Debrief:**
- Budgets are personal; no need to share publicly
- Move directly to Activity 3

**Differentiation:**
- **Struggling students:** Provide example allocation to start (they can modify it)
- **Advanced students:** "Calculate compound interest: If you invested $200/month at 8% annual return, what would you have after 5 years? 10 years?" (Ans: ~$14,700 after 5 years, ~$36,500 after 10 years using monthly compounding)

**Assessment Opportunity:**
During circulation, check: Are students connecting allocation decisions to opportunity costs? Can they articulate trade-offs?

### Activity 3: Incentive Design for Personal Goals (10 minutes)

**Objective:** Create practical incentive systems aligned with financial goals

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Introduction (2 minutes):**
1. "You're going to design an incentive system for yourself"
2. "Remember from yesterday: Incentives motivate behavior"
3. "Four types: Positive (rewards), Negative (consequences), Structural (make it easier), Social (peer pressure/support)"

**Independent Work (6 minutes):**
1. "Choose one financial goal from the list or create your own"
2. "Complete the Incentive Design table"
3. Set timer
4. Circulate and coach:
   - Are incentives realistic and specific?
   - Do they align with the goal?
   - Are they using multiple types of incentives?

**Examples of Good Incentive Design:**
- **Goal:** Save $2,000 in 6 months
- **Positive:** Every $500 saved → reward dinner out ($30 budget)
- **Negative:** Miss weekly target → no streaming service that week
- **Structural:** Auto-transfer $75/week to separate account (can't access easily)
- **Social:** Tell roommate goal; they check in every Friday

**Examples of Weak Incentive Design:**
- **Vague:** "Reward myself if I do well" (What's the trigger? What's the reward?)
- **Misaligned:** Goal is save money, reward is expensive vacation (undermines goal)
- **Unrealistic:** "If I fail, I'll give $500 to charity" (too harsh, won't enforce)

**Partner Discussion (2 minutes):**
1. "Share your incentive system with a partner"
2. "Give each other feedback: Is it realistic? Will it work?"
3. "Refine based on feedback"

**Monitoring:**
- Are students designing incentives they'll actually implement?
- Do they understand the difference between the four types?

**Key Teaching Point:**
"The best incentive system is one you'll actually use. It should be:
- **Specific:** Clear triggers and rewards
- **Realistic:** You can enforce it
- **Aligned:** Supports your goal (doesn't undermine it)
- **Motivating:** Matters enough to change your behavior"

### Activity 4: {{STATE_NAME}} Policy Incentives Analysis (10 minutes)

**Objective:** Understand how {{STATE_NAME}} creates incentives affecting personal finance

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Part A: Identify Policy Incentives (5 minutes):**
1. "Complete the table: Identify {{STATE_NAME}} policies and the incentives they create"
2. "You may need to research some of these quickly on your phone"
3. Provide guidance on where to look:
   - Education: {{STATE_NAME}} Department of Education website
   - Housing: HUD first-time homebuyer programs
   - Tax incentives: {{STATE_NAME}} Department of Revenue

**Circulate and Assist:**
- Help students find relevant policies
- Ensure they're identifying the incentive mechanism (not just describing the program)
- Guide them to think about personal impact

**Examples to Highlight:**
- **{{STATE_NAME}}'s minimum wage {{STATE_MIN_WAGE}}:** Creates incentive for employers to automate or reduce hours; creates incentive for workers to seek employment
- **529 college savings plans with tax benefits:** Incentivizes saving for education early
- **First-time homebuyer down payment assistance:** Reduces barrier to homeownership

**Part B: Analysis Questions (3 minutes):**
1. "Answer the three analysis questions about unintended consequences, tax incentives, and trade-offs"
2. These require critical thinking—there are no single right answers

**Small Group Discussion (2 minutes):**
1. Arrange students in groups of 3-4
2. "Share your answers to Question 2 (tax incentives): Who benefits most? How can you take advantage?"
3. Groups discuss briefly

**Key Discussion Points:**
- Tax incentives often benefit those who already have resources to invest/save
- Understanding these policies helps you minimize opportunity costs
- Every incentive has trade-offs—someone pays (through opportunity cost of other uses of government funds)

**Differentiation:**
- **Struggling students:** Provide pre-filled table with some policies identified; students just complete analysis
- **Advanced students:** "Research: Compare {{STATE_NAME}}'s tax policies to a neighboring state. How do different incentives affect migration patterns?"

### Closing Activity: Opportunity Cost Calculator - Skill Builder (5 minutes)

**Objective:** Synthesize learning with practical tool

**Facilitation Steps:**

**Introduction (1 minute):**
1. "For our closing activity, you'll use the framework we've built today to analyze a real decision you face"
2. "Choose from the list or pick your own decision"

**Independent Work (3 minutes):**
1. "Complete the Opportunity Cost Calculator for both options"
2. "Include explicit costs, opportunity costs, and intangible benefits"
3. "Make your choice and explain your reasoning"

**No Whole-Class Sharing:**
- These are personal decisions
- Students keep their completed calculators

**Brief Wrap-Up (1 minute):**
1. "You now have a systematic framework for analyzing financial decisions"
2. "Use this process for every major choice: List alternatives → Calculate explicit costs → Calculate opportunity costs → Consider intangibles → Decide"
3. "There's rarely one 'right' answer. It depends on your goals, values, and circumstances. The key is making *informed* choices."

**Assessment Opportunity:**
Collect (or have students submit digitally) their completed Opportunity Cost Calculators. This serves as summative assessment for the chapter.

**Rubric for Grading:**
- Explicit costs correctly identified (25%)
- Opportunity costs correctly calculated (25%)
- Intangible factors considered (25%)
- Clear reasoning for decision (25%)

## Differentiation Strategies

### For Struggling Students:
- Provide partially completed calculation tables
- Pair with stronger student for Activities 1-2
- Allow use of calculator throughout
- Reduce number of analysis questions (focus on 1-2 key questions rather than all)
- Provide worked examples for reference

### For Advanced Students:
- Add complexity: "Calculate net present value of each college path using 5% discount rate"
- Research extension: "Compare {{STATE_NAME}} public university ROI to neighboring states"
- Additional scenario: "High-earning trade path (electrician: $75K by year 10)"
- Policy analysis: "How do {{STATE_NAME}}'s incentive policies compare to neighboring states? What effects do you predict on population/business migration?"

### For ELL Students:
- Provide sentence frames for analysis questions
- Allow use of translation apps for complex terms
- Pair with bilingual peer if available
- Use visual aids showing calculations step-by-step
- Provide completed example for each activity type

### For Students with IEPs:
- Extended time on calculations
- Option to complete fewer scenarios but with more depth
- Graphic organizers for opportunity cost comparisons
- Audio recording option instead of written responses
- Access to pre-populated data tables

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment (During Class):
- **Circulation:** Monitor students' calculations and analysis during activities
- **Partner discussions:** Listen for quality of reasoning and understanding of trade-offs
- **Questioning:** Ask individuals to explain their reasoning during circulation

### Summative Assessment:
**Option 1: Completed Opportunity Cost Calculator (recommended)**
- Students submit their final calculator analysis
- Rubric: Explicit costs (25%), Opportunity costs (25%), Intangibles (25%), Reasoning (25%)

**Option 2: Written Reflection**
- Prompt: "Describe a major financial decision you'll face in the next 1-5 years. Using opportunity cost analysis, explain which option you'll choose and why. Include explicit costs, opportunity costs, and intangible factors."
- Length: 1-2 pages
- Rubric: Accuracy of cost calculations (40%), Depth of analysis (30%), Quality of reasoning (30%)

**Option 3: Case Study Analysis**
- Provide new scenario (not from today's activities)
- Students analyze opportunity costs and make recommendation
- Rubric: Completeness (25%), Accuracy (25%), Analysis quality (25%), Recommendation clarity (25%)

## Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge 1: Students struggle with math/calculations**
- **Solution:** Have answer key ready. Allow students to work in pairs. Emphasize that the conceptual understanding matters more than perfect math.

**Challenge 2: Activities run long, timing is off**
- **Solution:** Have clear timers visible. Warn students: "2 minutes left." Be willing to move on even if some students aren't finished—they can complete as homework.

**Challenge 3: Students make unrealistic budget allocations**
- **Solution:** That's fine! The point is to practice the analysis. During circulation, ask: "Is this realistic? Would you actually do this?" Help them refine.

**Challenge 4: Low engagement with policy analysis (Activity 4)**
- **Solution:** Use recent {{STATE_NAME}} news (minimum wage debate, tax proposal, housing crisis). Make it current and relevant. Connect to students' actual experiences.

**Challenge 5: Students finish at very different paces**
- **Solution:** Have extension questions ready for fast finishers. Or allow them to start on homework/next topic while others complete.
