# L-57 Teacher Guide: Loan Application Process and Requirements - Day 1

## Lesson Overview
This lesson introduces students to the comprehensive process of applying for loans, understanding documentation requirements, and strengthening their creditworthiness to secure approval on favorable terms. Students will learn the Five C's framework lenders use to evaluate applications, explore required documentation for different loan types, and develop strategies to prepare strong applications. The lesson emphasizes practical preparation—students leave with concrete knowledge about what information they'll need, how lenders assess applications, and specific actions they can take to improve their approval odds. The Loan Application Simulator skill builder provides hands-on practice completing realistic applications and seeing how different factors affect outcomes.

## Learning Objectives
- Complete a comprehensive loan application with accuracy and required documentation
- Identify and gather the necessary documentation for different types of loan applications
- Understand how lenders evaluate creditworthiness and assess loan applications
- Analyze factors that increase or decrease likelihood of loan approval
- Develop strategies to strengthen loan applications and improve approval odds

### Facilitation Notes
This lesson deals with content that may make students uncomfortable—many come from families who have struggled with loan applications, faced denials, or accepted predatory terms due to lack of understanding. Create a judgment-free environment that acknowledges these realities while empowering students with knowledge. Some students may have no credit history yet, which can feel discouraging when discussing approval odds; emphasize that starting from zero is normal and that building credit is a learnable skill. Others may have parents with poor credit and fear they're destined for the same; stress that credit is not inherited and that understanding these processes can break negative patterns.

## Detailed Lesson Plan

### Introduction & Hook (5 minutes)
**0:00-0:05**

**Activity:** Present the following scenario:

"Imagine you've just landed a great job 30 miles from home and public transportation isn't an option. You need a reliable car within two weeks. You find a perfect certified pre-owned sedan for $22,000 and have $3,000 saved for a down payment. You walk into the dealership and they say, 'Great choice! Let's get you financed.' They hand you a multi-page application asking for your employment history, income details, every debt you owe, bank account information, and your Social Security number. You're not sure what half the terms mean. They'll check your credit, calculate ratios you've never heard of, and contact your employer. You feel completely lost and worried you'll make a mistake that costs you the car or locks you into a bad loan. How do you feel in this moment?"

**Facilitation:**
- Allow students to share feelings: overwhelmed, anxious, unprepared, intimidated
- Acknowledge that loan applications can be daunting, especially the first time
- Explain that this discomfort often leads people to either (a) accept whatever terms are offered without questioning, or (b) avoid borrowing even when it makes financial sense
- **Key message**: "Today we're demystifying this process. By the end of class, you'll know exactly what information lenders want, why they want it, how they evaluate you, and what you can do to prepare strong applications that get approved on favorable terms."

**Transition:** "Let's start by defining the key terms you'll encounter on loan applications and in the approval process."

---

### Key Concepts Review (10 minutes)
**0:05-0:15**

**Activity:** Review the 10 key concepts, ensuring students understand each term and can apply it to real situations.

**Facilitation Strategy:**
Rather than simply reading definitions, use a participatory approach:
- Display each term
- Ask students to predict what it means before providing the definition
- Provide a real-world example for each term
- Connect terms to students' likely experiences

**Critical Concepts to Emphasize:**

**Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI):**
- This is one of the most important calculations lenders make
- Work through a live example: "$3,500 monthly income, $800 in existing debt payments. What's the DTI?" (22.9%)
- Explain that most lenders prefer DTI below 36% before adding a new loan, though some allow up to 43%
- Connect to real impact: "If your DTI is too high, you'll be denied even with good credit"

**Hard Inquiry:**
- Many students don't know that applying for credit temporarily lowers their score
- Explain the exception: multiple applications for the same type of loan within 14-45 days count as one inquiry (allows rate shopping)
- Warn against scattershot approach: "Don't apply to five different lenders randomly—your score drops 5-10 points for each inquiry"

**Co-signer:**
- Critical for students with limited credit history
- Emphasize the seriousness: "If you fail to pay, your co-signer's credit is destroyed and they're legally responsible"
- Many young adults ask parents to co-sign without fully understanding the risk they're imposing

**Underwriting:**
- This is the "black box" that intimidates borrowers
- Demystify: "It's just a systematic process of verifying everything you've told them and calculating risk"
- Typically takes 1-7 days for most loans, 30-45 days for mortgages

**Check for Understanding:**
- Quick quiz: "Your friend makes $4,000/month and has $600 in student loan payments, $200 car payment, and $100 in minimum credit card payments. They want to borrow $15,000 for a car with a $350/month payment. What's their current DTI? What would it be with the new loan? Would a lender approve?" (Current: 22.5%; With new loan: 31.25%; Likely approved)

**Transition:** "Now that we understand the language of loan applications, let's explore how lenders actually evaluate whether to approve you and on what terms."

### Deeper Exploration (15 minutes)
**0:15-0:30**

This section covers four major topics. Manage time carefully to address each adequately.

#### Part 1: Different Loan Types and Requirements (4 minutes)
**0:15-0:19**

**Facilitation:**
- Create a comparison chart on the board with four loan types: Auto, Personal, Mortgage, Student
- For each, highlight:
  - Typical amounts
  - Secured vs. unsecured
  - Approval timeline
  - Key documentation requirements
  - Difficulty of approval (easiest to hardest)

**Key Points:**
- Auto loans are often students' first major credit experience—secured by the vehicle, moderate approval standards
- Personal loans are hardest to get (unsecured, high risk for lender)
- Mortgages require most extensive documentation but have competitive rates (secured, long relationship)
- Federal student loans are unique—based on need, not creditworthiness

**Activity:**
Ask students: "Which loan type do you think you're most likely to apply for first? Why?" This personalizes the content and helps you gauge student circumstances.

#### Part 2: The Five C's of Credit (5 minutes)
**0:19-0:24**

**Facilitation:**
This framework is the lesson's core. Create a visual showing all five C's:

1. **Character** (Credit History)
2. **Capacity** (Ability to Repay)
3. **Capital** (Financial Resources)
4. **Collateral** (Security for Secured Loans)
5. **Conditions** (Loan Purpose & Economic Environment)

**Teaching Strategy:**
For each C, provide:
- Clear definition
- What lenders are assessing
- How students can strengthen this area
- Quick example

**Character Example:**
"Character is your track record. Credit scores quantify this. Someone with a 760 score has proven they pay bills on time for years. Someone with a 580 score has late payments, collections, or defaults. Lenders trust high scores because past behavior predicts future behavior."

**Capacity Example:**
"Capacity is simply: Can you afford this payment? Lenders calculate DTI. If you make $3,000/month and already pay $900 in debts, adding a $600 car payment puts you at 50% DTI—that's too high, you can't afford it regardless of your credit score."

**Capital Example:**
"Capital is your financial cushion. For a $300,000 mortgage, would you rather lend to someone with $15,000 down payment and $5,000 in savings, or someone with $60,000 down and $30,000 in savings? The second borrower is less risky—they have reserves if income is disrupted."

**Interactive Element:**
Present a borrower profile: "Credit score 680, makes $50,000/year with $8,000 in existing debt payments, has $6,000 saved, wants to buy a $25,000 car. Which C's are strong? Which are weak?" (Character: moderate; Capacity: good—DTI 16% without new loan; Capital: low for down payment; Collateral: will be okay with car as security; Conditions: appropriate purchase for income level)

#### Part 3: Required Documentation (3 minutes)
**0:24-0:27**

**Facilitation:**
Many students will be overwhelmed by documentation requirements. Normalize this: "Yes, it's a lot of paperwork. That's WHY we're learning this now—so when you apply for real, you're not scrambling."

**Create a checklist students can reference:**
- Government ID & Social Security number
- Recent pay stubs (2-3 months)
- W-2 from previous year (or tax returns if self-employed)
- Bank statements (2-3 months)
- Current debt statements
- For secured loans: information about collateral (vehicle VIN, property address)

**Emphasize:**
- Self-employed borrowers face additional scrutiny (2 years of tax returns, profit/loss statements)
- Large deposits require explanations—lenders verify down payments aren't borrowed money
- Employment is verified directly—don't exaggerate income or employment length

**Real-World Advice:**
"Start organizing these documents now, even if you're not applying for a loan soon. Create a financial documents folder—digital or physical—where you keep pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements. When you need to apply for a loan, you'll have everything ready instead of frantically searching for paperwork."

#### Part 4: Strategies to Strengthen Applications (3 minutes)
**0:27-0:30**

**Facilitation:**
This section empowers students with actionable strategies. Focus on tactics they can implement immediately.

**Six Strategies:**

1. **Improve credit scores before applying** - Even 20-40 point improvements can shift rate tiers
2. **Reduce DTI** - Pay off small debts before adding new ones
3. **Increase down payments** - Shows financial discipline, reduces lender risk
4. **Maintain employment stability** - Avoid job changes during application process if possible
5. **Consider co-signers strategically** - Use only when necessary, with full understanding of implications
6. **Get pre-approved** - Provides leverage and clarifies budget before shopping

**Real Impact Example:**
"On a $300,000 mortgage, the difference between a 6.5% rate and a 7% rate is $100/month, or $36,000 over 30 years. Six months of credit improvement could save $36,000. That's why preparation matters."

**Interactive Element:**
"If you were applying for a loan six months from now, which of these six strategies could you realistically implement starting today?" Have students identify at least one action they could take.

**Transition:** "These strategies aren't theoretical. Let's see how they worked—or didn't work—for three real borrowers."

### Real-World Examples (10 minutes)
**0:30-0:40**

**Activity:** Analyze three borrower scenarios with different levels of preparation and dramatically different outcomes.

**Facilitation Strategy:**
Don't just read the examples. Create engaging discussion around each one.

#### Example 1: Carmen's Prepared Auto Loan Application (3 minutes)

**Key Points:**
- Started preparing 3 months before shopping
- Identified and fixed credit issues (late payments, errors)
- Gathered documentation in advance
- Got pre-approval from credit union
- Used pre-approval to negotiate better dealer rates
- **Result:** Saved $1,400 over 5 years through preparation

**Discussion Questions:**
- What did Carmen do that most borrowers skip? (checked credit months in advance, disputed errors, organized documentation)
- Why did having credit union pre-approval help at the dealership? (leverage, proof of competing offer)
- How realistic is 3 months of preparation? (very realistic for planned purchases)

**Key Lesson:** "Preparation doesn't just increase approval odds—it saves real money through better rates."

#### Example 2: Marcus's Unprepared Personal Loan Struggle (3 minutes)

**Key Points:**
- Applied reactively without checking credit first
- Applied to multiple lenders in one day (multiple hard inquiries)
- Credit score lower than expected (610), high utilization (85%)
- Scrambled to gather documentation, delaying process
- Omitted job change information, triggering additional scrutiny
- Accepted 22% rate (barely better than credit cards he was trying to consolidate)
- **Result:** Paid $6,000-9,000 more than necessary over loan life

**Discussion Questions:**
- What mistakes did Marcus make? (didn't check credit first, applied everywhere simultaneously, wasn't honest about job change, accepted bad terms out of desperation)
- How could 3-6 months of preparation have changed his outcome? (improve credit score, reduce utilization, stabilize at new job)
- Why was 22% interest barely helpful for debt consolidation? (credit cards are typically 18-25%, so minimal savings)

**Key Lesson:** "Desperation leads to bad terms. Reactive applications cost thousands of dollars. Preparation and patience pay off."

#### Example 3: Jasmine's Strategic Mortgage Pre-Approval (3 minutes)

**Key Points:**
- Started planning a year before house shopping
- Identified high DTI (38%) and created improvement plan
- Paid off car loan (freed up $285/month capacity)
- Reduced credit card balances below 30% utilization
- Credit score improved from 720 to 745
- Gathered extensive documentation before applying
- Shopped multiple lenders through broker
- Chose 15% down instead of 10% to eliminate PMI and reduce rate
- Pre-approval gave negotiating power with sellers
- **Result:** Saved $45,000 over 30 years through strategic preparation

**Discussion Questions:**
- What's the most impressive part of Jasmine's strategy? (year-long planning, specific DTI improvement, documentation preparation)
- How did larger down payment (15% vs. 10%) help? (eliminated $200/month PMI, reduced interest rate)
- Why did pre-approval help her win the house? (sellers trust buyers with financing secured)

**Key Lesson:** "Strategic preparation for major loans can save tens of thousands of dollars. The time invested in improvement pays huge returns."

**Comparative Discussion (1 minute):**
"Which borrower's approach is closest to how you'd naturally handle a loan application? Which approach do you want to adopt after today's lesson?"

**Transition:** "Now let's think about your own financial situation and future borrowing needs."

### Reflection Discussion (5 minutes)
**0:40-0:45**

**Activity:** Students consider their anticipated borrowing needs and assess their current creditworthiness.

**Facilitation:**
This can be sensitive territory. Some students may not want to share financial details publicly. Offer options:
1. Written individual reflection (private)
2. Think-pair-share with a partner
3. Anonymous sharing (write on cards, teacher reads aloud)
4. Opt-out option with alternative assignment

**Reflection Prompt:**
"Think about a major loan you might need within the next 2-5 years (auto loan, student loan, personal loan, or mortgage). Assess your current creditworthiness using the Five C's framework:
- **Character**: How is your credit history (or if you have none yet, what's your plan to build it)?
- **Capacity**: What's your current or anticipated income? Any existing debts?
- **Capital**: Are you building savings? How much do you have or could you have in 2-5 years?
- **Collateral**: For secured loans, what might you use?
- **Conditions**: Does the loan purpose align with your financial goals?

Based on this honest assessment, what specific actions could you take in the next 6-12 months to strengthen your future loan application?"

**Facilitation Notes:**
- Many students will have limited or no credit history—frame this as an opportunity, not a deficit
- Some may have poor family credit situations—emphasize that credit is individual, not inherited
- Focus on actionable next steps rather than dwelling on current limitations

**If Using Discussion Format:**
Prompt students: "Who's willing to share one action they could take in the next 6 months to strengthen a future loan application?" (Examples: become authorized user on parent's card, open secured credit card, start saving $50/month, pay off a small debt)

**Transition:** "Let's put this knowledge into practice with a tool that simulates the loan application process."

**Circulate and Assist:**

## Differentiation Strategies

### For Advanced Students
- Challenge them to complete applications for all three loan types and compare documentation requirements
- Ask them to calculate the interest savings from different credit score improvements (e.g., what's the dollar impact of improving from 680 to 740 on a $250,000 mortgage?)
- Have them research state-specific lending regulations or consumer protection laws
- Assign exploration of specialized loan types (FHA mortgages, VA loans, construction loans)

### For Struggling Students
- Provide a completed sample application as a reference
- Create a simplified version of the Five C's framework focusing on just three: Credit History, Income/Debts, and Savings
- Allow extra time on the Skill Builder with one-on-one support
- Focus on just one loan type (auto loans, as most accessible first borrowing experience)
- Provide a documentation checklist template they can keep and reference

### For English Language Learners
- Pre-teach vocabulary: collateral, underwriting, creditworthiness, debt-to-income ratio
- Provide loan application glossary with definitions in both English and native language if possible
- Use visual aids extensively: charts, diagrams showing application process flow
- Allow use of translation tools when working with Loan Application Simulator
- Pair with English-proficient partners for discussions

### For Diverse Learners
- Acknowledge that financial systems and credit histories vary globally; immigrant students may be starting from zero for U.S. credit
- Recognize that family financial literacy levels vary; avoid assumptions that all students have family guidance
- Provide examples that span income levels and life situations (not just traditional paths)
- Offer alternative ways to demonstrate understanding beyond written responses (verbal explanations, diagrams, etc.)

## Common Misconceptions and Challenges

### Misconception 1: "Checking my credit hurts my score"
**Reality:** Checking your own credit (soft inquiry) does not affect your score. Only lender inquiries (hard inquiries) as part of applications affect scores, and even those have minimal impact (5-10 points temporarily).

**How to Address:** Encourage students to check their credit reports regularly (free annually at annualcreditreport.com). Explain the difference between soft and hard inquiries clearly.

### Misconception 2: "I can't get a loan without credit history"
**Reality:** While challenging, first-time borrowers can get loans through several methods: secured loans (where collateral reduces risk), credit-builder loans, co-signers, or starting with smaller amounts (secured credit cards, small auto loans).

**How to Address:** Provide concrete pathways for building credit from zero. Emphasize that everyone starts somewhere, and strategic credit-building over 6-12 months opens doors.

### Misconception 3: "My parents' bad credit affects me"
**Reality:** Credit is individual. Parents' credit scores don't affect their children's scores unless the child is a co-signer or authorized user on parent accounts. Each person builds their own credit history.

**How to Address:** Explicitly state this to alleviate anxiety for students from families with financial struggles. Emphasize that they have the power to build strong credit regardless of family history.

### Misconception 4: "The lowest monthly payment is the best deal"
**Reality:** Lowest monthly payments often come from longest loan terms, resulting in far more total interest paid. A $400/month payment over 72 months costs much more than a $500/month payment over 48 months.

**How to Address:** Always calculate and compare total cost (principal + interest) across different term lengths. Show specific examples of how longer terms inflate total cost.

### Challenge: Students with No Income or Credit
Some students are not yet employed and have no credit history, making discussions of DTI and credit scores feel irrelevant.

**How to Address:** Frame this as preparation for near-future needs (within 2-3 years). Discuss specific actions they can take now: become authorized user on family member's card, open a secured credit card with a small amount, understand the process before they need it. Position knowledge as power that serves them when they do need credit.

### Challenge: Students Facing Predatory Lending in Their Communities
Some students come from communities targeted by payday lenders, title loan companies, and other predatory operations.

**How to Address:** Explicitly discuss predatory lending practices: excessive fees, very high interest rates (200%+ APR), confusing terms, aggressive collections. Provide resources for legitimate alternatives (credit unions, nonprofit financial counseling). Emphasize that understanding standard loan application processes helps you recognize when someone's trying to take advantage.

## Assessment Opportunities

### Formative Assessment
- **During Key Concepts:** Quick check calculations (DTI problems, impact of credit score on rates)
- **During Real-World Examples:** Ask students to identify what each borrower did well and where they made mistakes
- **During Skill Builder:** Observe student work, check that they're completing applications accurately and understanding feedback

### Summative Assessment (for end of two-day sequence)
- Complete a loan application accurately using a provided borrower profile
- Calculate DTI and LTV ratios correctly
- Identify required documentation for a specific loan type
- Develop a 6-month creditworthiness improvement plan for a given borrower scenario
- Explain how the Five C's apply to a specific loan application

### Alternative Assessments
- **Portfolio Approach:** Students create a "Loan Application Readiness Portfolio" with documentation checklist, credit improvement plan, and completed sample application
- **Case Study Analysis:** Evaluate three borrower applications and make approve/deny recommendations with justification
- **Peer Teaching:** Students create a guide for a specific loan type (auto, personal, student, or mortgage) explaining requirements and strategies
